Life After Roe:
The Uncertain Future of Abortion Rights

By: Ellie Jornlin

For nearly 50 years...

For nearly 50 years, Roe v. Wade protected the right to a safe, legal abortion in the United States at the federal level. However, in 2022, with the Supreme Court ruling in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, abortion is no longer considered protected under the U.S. Constitution and is left up to the states to decide.

Abortion Bans Across the Country

Twenty-one states now ban or restrict abortions earlier in pregnancy than the standard set by Roe v. Wade, which governed reproductive rights for nearly a century before.

Credit: New York Times


Abortion is legal in the rest of the country, and several states have even added new protections since Dobbs.

Credit: New York Times


Obstetricians are leaving the states that have put bans in place as they cannot provide the necessary care to their patients, even women facing life-threatening pregnancy complications. Maternal and infant deaths have increased since pregnant people have been forced to carry pregnancies to term with no chance of survival, at risk to their own health and survival.


Additionally, abortion bans disproportionately affect poor women and women of color, further contributing to systemic inequalities by unequal limitation of their access to essential healthcare.

Photo Credit: National Partnership for Women & Families

Threats to Reproductive Freedoms from an Anti-Rights Administration

Following the election of Donald Trump, the threats to reproductive freedom have only grown larger. Trump has spoken openly about his views on abortion and his goals to ban or limit abortion access. During his 2016 campaign, he pledged to appoint Supreme Court justices who would help overturn Roe v. Wade, and as president, he accomplished that goal.

The Trump administration has released Project 2025, a detailed plan outlining the goals for the next four years.

Project 2025: The Effect on Abortion Access

  • Threats to Medication Abortion: Mifepristone is an extremely safe and effective medication used commonly for medication abortion in the United States.
    • Project 2025 outlines reinstating restrictions on mifepristone that require in-person dispensing and limiting who can prescribe and receive access to it.
    • The plan also recommends revoking the US Food and Drug Administration's approval for mifepristone, which would remove it from the market entirely.
    • Denying access to this drug would increase in-person abortion services which would place additional strain on clinics.
    • Many patients will also resort to unsafe abortion methods.
  • Broader Attacks on Abortion Access: Codifying into law the Hyde and Weldon Amendments, which restrict the use of federal funds for abortion care.
    • Project 2025 seeks to codify into law the Hyde and Weldon Amendments, which restrict the use of federal funds for abortion care.
  • Denying Access to Emergency Abortion Care: The plan also calls for the Department of Health and Human Services to dismantle abortion protections under the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act.
    • This would eliminate protections for people needing abortion services as a part of emergency treatment
    • It would also end investigations into cases where patients’ rights were violated through the denial of medically necessary emergency abortion care.
  • Weaponization of Medicaid: Project 2025 calls for the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services to encourage states to eliminate Planned Parenthood facilities from their state Medicaid programs.
    • This would have extremely negative effects on basic healthcare services across the country, especially on low-income families.
  • Contraception: The plan hopes to undermine Title X, the national publicly funded family planning program, and the federal contraceptive coverage guarantee of the Affordable Care Act.
    • This plan would make it easier for employers to exclude contraceptive coverage from their employees’ health plan.
    • It would also prohibit crucial funding from going to entities that perform or support abortion care.

While we don’t know for sure if these plans will be put into place during Trump’s time in office, the possibility alone underscores a chilling reality for reproductive rights in America, leaving many fearful about what the future holds.
